Understand Railway Derailment Causes

A d3.js data visualization project using Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) Rail Equipment Accident/Incident (REA) Data
Xinhao Liu - UIUC CS416 Data Visualization

About the Visualization

This visualization is an interactive slideshow that takes the user through the Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) Rail Equipment Accident/Incident (REA) Data, focusing on introducing the five major accident cause categories from both frequency and severity perspectives using three visualizations.


About the Dataset

The FRA REA dataset includes information on all rail accidents where the total monetary damage to track, equipment, or structures exceeds an FRA-specified monetary threshold. This threshold is periodically adjusted for inflation and is $12,000 in 2024. While the FRA REA dataset contains all types of train accidents, such as those involving freight trains, passenger trains, and yard/switching trains, and all types of accidents, including derailments, collisions, and grade-crossing accidents, this visualization focuses on Class I railroads freight train derailment accidents happend on mainline and siding tracks between 1997-2023. However, users can still freely explore the distribution characteristics among the five categories by interacting with the webpage.


Template Scene

The use of CSS and FullPage.js provides a unified template for the interactive visualization. Each slide transitions in a consistent manner, maintaining uniform text elements (font size and family). Additionally, a navigation bar on the right side allows users to easily switch between slides. Tooltips appear when users hover over the navigation, adjusting the display parameters for each menu item.


Annotations

Annotations have been strategically used across all three visualizations to highlight key data points and enhance interpretability. For the scatter plot visualization, annotations identify the average frequency and severity, and divided all causes into four quadrants. These annotations appear in the background of the plot with a lighter gray color, Similarly, the bar plots utilize annotations to mark significant data points, such as the highest total number of cars derailed and cumulative percentage values, offering detailed insights directly on the visual elements.


Parameters and Triggers

Interactive parameters and triggers are integral to all three visualizations, enhancing user engagement and data exploration. In the scatter plot visualization, parameters for x and y coordinates are initially set to display average frequency and severity. As users hover over different points, tooltips are triggered, revealing detailed information about each derailment cause. Additionally, selecting different categories through the interactive legend filters the data points displayed, dynamically adjusting the visual representation.


The bar plots incorporate similar interactive features. For the plot showing the total number of cars derailed, x-axis parameters are set to the different derailment causes, while the y-axis represents the total number of cars. Hovering over the bars triggers tooltips that display specific details about each cause, including a percentage. An additional secondary y-axis is used to visualize the cumulative percentage with a purple line, updating in real-time as users interact with the plot. The interactive legend allows users to filter the data by category, changing the color and visibility of the bars accordingly.


These interactive elements and annotations collectively enhance the user's ability to understand and analyze the data, providing a comprehensive view of the derailment causes from multiple perspectives. The use of parameters and triggers ensures that the visualizations remain dynamic and responsive, allowing for an intuitive exploration of the dataset.

FRA Derailment Cause Categories

There are five major derailment cause categories, and under each category there are many cause groups. AAR-ADL developed 51 cause groups that better reflect the engineering and operational relationships between different causes.

Track, Roadbed, and Structure: This category includes causes related to the condition of the track and roadbed structures.
Cause groups examples: Broken Rails or Welds, Joint Bar Defects, Buckled Track

Signals and Communication: This category includes causes related to signaling systems and communication issues.
Cause groups example: Signal Failures

Human Factors: This category includes causes related to human errors in train operation.
Cause groups examples: Brake Operation, Handbrake Operations, Train Handling

Equipment: This category includes causes related to mechanical and electrical failures in the train.
Cause groups examples: Centerplate/Carbody Defects, Coupler Defects, Broken Wheels

Miscellaneous: This category includes other miscellaneous causes that do not fit into the above categories.
Cause groups examples: Obstructions, Grade Crossing Collisions, Extreme Weather

Cause Frequency vs. Cause Severity

The Frequency vs. Severity plot is the first visualization in this interactive slideshow that introduces the causes of derailments. Presented by Dick et al. (2003) and Barkan et al. (2003), each derailment cause is plotted in terms of its average frequency and average severity. Dashed lines divide the graph into four quadrants: the horizontal line represents the average severity for all causes, and the vertical line represents the average frequency for all causes. Therefore, points to the right of the vertical line indicate above-average frequency, and points above the horizontal line indicate above-average severity. As shown, Broken Rails or Welds is the most frequent cause of derailments, while Other Rail and Joint Defects is the most severe cause, derailing an average of 18.5 cars.

Total Number of Cars Derailed vs. Causes

Besides using the average number of cars derailed to study the severity of derailment causes, focusing on the total number of cars derailed and the total monetary damage cost are two other commonly used metrics. In this slide, we delve into the total number of cars derailed. Similar to the previous slide, Broken Rails or Welds again accounts for the largest number of derailed cars among all causes. Extreme Weather ranks second, followed by Buckled Track. However, Other Rail and Joint Defects, which was identified as "the most severe" derailment cause in the previous slide, only accounts for 2.3% of all derailed cars, ranking 14th among all causes.

Total Damage Cost vs. Causes

The next metric used to measure the severity of derailment causes is the total monetary damage cost. This slide explores this aspect. Broken Rails or Welds again causes the highest monetary damage, as expected. However, the second highest damage is caused by Broken Wheels, and the third by Extreme Weather. This highlights the importance of focusing on various metrics to fully understand the underlying nature of each derailment cause.

Findings

In conclusion, this interactive slideshow introduces three common visualizations to help viewers understand derailment causes from both frequency and severity perspectives. All derailment causes are grouped into five major categories and 51 cause groups. Each major category is consistently color-coded throughout the entire slideshow. Viewers can easily grasp the overall picture from the visualizations: Broken Rails or Welds is the most frequent and severe derailment cause by all definitions. By interacting with the legend, viewers can also learn that derailments caused by Track issues are generally more frequent and severe, while those caused by Human Factors and Equipment are less common on average, but their severity varies from cause to cause.

Reminder

Lastly, this interactive slideshow can serve as a reference for understanding derailment causes in general. Many research studies focus on specific derailment causes, and conclusions can vary based on different contexts. Please be mindful and open-minded if you encounter different conclusions in academic papers on derailment causes and severity.
